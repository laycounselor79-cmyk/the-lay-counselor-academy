# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Adults stepping into the role of helping other people through hard moments without being clinicians. Concretely: community helpers, AA-style sponsors, crisis-line and warm-line volunteers, school peer-counselors, RAs, HR peer-listeners, faith-community lay listeners (secular framing), affinity-group facilitators.

Their context when they arrive: they've already said yes to a role, or are considering it, and feel under-equipped. They're motivated but cautious — afraid of saying the wrong thing, of making it worse, of being a fraud. Many are doing this on top of a day job or unpaid. They're evaluating whether this academy is serious, kind, and real before they trust it with money and time.

The job they're trying to get done: become a more confident, less harmful listener — and feel like someone who studies their craft, not someone who took a weekend course.

## Product Purpose

The Lay Counselor Academy is a paid training program for peer counselors that produces skilled, humble, well-supervised listeners. The marketing surface exists to convert qualified, motivated learners into paid subscribers and keep them oriented through the program. Success at 12 months is recurring revenue from people who finish, recommend it, and stay.

## Brand Personality

Three words: **steady, literary, kind.**

Voice: like a respected teacher who has done the work for twenty years and has nothing to prove. Speaks in full sentences. Uses real examples instead of slogans. Never closes; invites. Believes the reader is an adult.

Emotional goals: when someone lands on the page, they should feel *settled* (not hyped), *taken seriously* (not pitched), and *curious to read more* (not pressured to click). The dominant feeling is the one you get walking into a good independent bookstore — quiet authority, warmth, things made carefully.

Reference lane: warm-editorial. Closer to *The Marginalian*, *On Being*, *Aeon*, a thoughtful literary nonfiction imprint, the editorial side of Headspace, the design language of a well-funded magazine — than to any SaaS marketing site.

## Anti-references

The single strongest one to avoid: **self-help / course-bro energy.** No big-promise headlines ("Become a counselor in 30 days"), no aggressive sales CTAs, no countdown timers, no fake scarcity, no all-caps urgency, no "as seen in" logo soup, no testimonial cards with stock-photo faces, no influencer-style portraits, no hero-metric template ("10,000 students • 4.9★ • 50hrs").

Also avoid: generic SaaS marketing-template look (purple gradients, identical feature cards), clinical/medical/insurance-portal aesthetic (cold blue, sterile, hospital-formy), and crisis-heavy charity tone (muted greys, sad-face stock photography, "people are suffering" guilt framing). This work is serious, but the page should not be heavy.

## Design Principles

1. **Practice the craft we teach.** Peer counseling is patient, present, and non-coercive. The site must be the same — no pressure tactics, no manipulative copy, no dark patterns. If a UI move would feel pushy in a counseling room, it's wrong here.
2. **Editorial over marketing.** Treat this like a publication that happens to sell a program. Long-form writing, real examples, named voices. The reader should be able to spend twenty quiet minutes here and feel they got something even if they don't buy yet.
3. **Calm is the conversion lever.** For this audience and topic, trust beats urgency. Slowness, whitespace, and visible craft signal credibility more than any badge or testimonial would.
4. **Adult, not aspirational.** Speak to people who have already accepted real responsibility. No transformation fantasies, no "your best self." Concrete skills, honest limits, named teachers.
5. **Human texture, not heroic portraits.** Warmth comes from specific detail — a quoted line from a session, a teacher's handwriting, a real classroom — not from polished testimonial faces or stock smiles.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 AA across the marketing site, with calm-by-default motion as a design assumption: animations should feel like opt-in, not opt-out. Treat `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` as the baseline experience; the motion-on variant adds gentle, slow easing only. Body type at comfortable reading size (≥17px equivalent) with generous line-height; plain-language copy throughout, since some of the audience are non-native English readers and many are stressed when they arrive. Strong, visible focus states; full keyboard nav; alt text on every meaningful image; no information conveyed by color alone.
